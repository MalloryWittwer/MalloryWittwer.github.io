A software for decoding 3D printed information in microstructures

During my time as a Research Associate in the Additive Microstructure Engineering Laboratory at NTU in Singapore, my team was interested in using 3D printing of metals to create new types of functionally graded materials. This technology is not yet mature; however, it has potential to significantly change the way we design technological parts in the future. The idea is that, during the 3D priming process, we control parameters such as the laser power or scanning speed to influence the microstructure of the material (for example, the morphology and orientation of the grains). In this way, we are able to build blocks with different properties within a single printed part, in a way similar to a LEGO structure.

At the time, I was involved in the characterization of our printed functionally graded materials. In our tests, we were including two types of microstructures as rectangular blocks. To validate the tests, we needed to quickly check that the blocks were in the right place, with the right microstructure. For this, we were using a directional reflectance technique to provide a sequence of images of the printed surface, from which a segmentation into two classes (microstructures 1 and 2) had to be retrieved. There were many possible ways to build the segmentation pipeline involving solutions that landed more on the soft- or hard-coding side. In this case, I opted for an approach that involved decomposing the signal using Principal Component Analysis (PCA) and feeding the PCA projection to a shallow binary neural network classifier. I found that this approach offered the best combination of high performance and lightweight computation.

The decoding technology worked, more or less, but the program had to be used by other members of my team who were not familiar with coding. To make it easy for my collaborators to use my program, I integrated to it a Graphical User Interface (GUI) coded in Tkinter (Python). To compile the app, the program involved bringing together fifteen different elements (displays, menus, windows…) that fulfilled different functions. The app was executed from app.py at the top of the directory structure. I also included a setup.py, README and license file and backed up the project on GitHub.

The GUI featured a primary display of the microstructure complemented by two side displays that showed the reflectance signal at the mouse cursor location and various other information. On the right were specialized panels for data processing and analysis. These panels were actually modular; I designed several of them which could be included for various studies. Here, in the first panel, resolution of the dataset could be reduced, and the field of view cropped, to reduce memory requirement. The second panel was for binary classification of microstructural features. This panel let the user tailor the segmentation pipeline to quickly iterate over different approaches, optimize hyperparameters, visualize and save the results. It included options to choose which dimensionality reduction technique to apply (PCA, NMF, None) and to customize the neural network architecture (number of nodes and layers, regularization, activation function…). Selection of a training set, training and prediction of microstructural classes were also accessible from within the GUI.

Overall, I enjoyed working on that project and learnt a lot. It was the biggest Python project I had to manage so far, in terms of the volume of code to manage. I had to think quite hard in order to make the project structure manageable and keep the code simple and understandable, while still enabling many complex backend interactions between graphical elements, data, and machine learning models. Moreover, particularly enjoyed thinking about the user experience which I wanted to be comfortable and intuitive. The final program was running smoothly and enabled faster research iterations on 3D printed functionally graded materials, which is likely to boost research outputs in this field.
