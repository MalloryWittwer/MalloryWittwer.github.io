Summarizing an issue as complex as decarbonization and the potential avenues to solve it in 5 minutes is challenging but worthwhile! We should always keep the big picture in mind before diving into the details. This is the first article of a series intended to provide a compass to navigate through the landscape of our climate crisis, understand it, and move forward towards a sustainable society.

The problem of global warming originates from that, in the current situation, human standards of living, the GDP, energy consumption, fossil fuels extraction, CO2 emissions, and the average temperature of the Earth relative to pre-industrial levels form a chain of correlated variables that all follow each other overtime. In fact, these variables also cause each other: temperature rises because we emit CO2 because we burn fossil fuels because we use energy because we need energy to produce things because we have needs and desires to satisfy.

Therefore, if we set as an objective to maintain high standards of living across the world and at the same time limit global warming, then we must take decisions on how to break that chain (to decouple the variables. There are several ways to achieve that:

§  Reducing energy intensity: We keep producing goods and services but use less energy.

§  Reducing carbon intensity: We keep using energy but produce it from low-carbon sources.

§  Carbon capture and storage: We keep burning fossil fuels but reduce CO2 release.

§  Carbon removal: We remove CO2 from the atmosphere permanently.

§  Geoengineering: We keep releasing CO2 into the atmosphere but avoid global warming as a result.

From there, the challenge is to figure out which of these approaches are worth pursuing and to what extent, in a given context. This involves evaluating and managing the costs, benefits, uncertainties, and risks associated with each option – an aspect heavily influenced by existing technologies and market incentives. Let’s have a quick look at each approach.

1 Reducing energy intensity (Energy / GDP)

Energy efficiency represents how much energy is required to generate a unit of GDP. Reducing it means reducing the energy cost associated with providing a product or service. This can be achieved by minimizing the energy wasted in operations and processes and by optimizing the use of energy. We can get smarter about the management of existing systems, supply, and production chains, and by making products more energy efficient. Steel production, for example, accounts for about 8 percent of the world’s CO2 emissions [1]. There is a minimum energy required to reduce iron ore for making steel which is of about 7 MJ/kg [2] (the free energy of formation) while current processes produce iron at around 25 MJ/kg [3] (the embodied energy). This leaves a potentially significant room to improve efficiency. An increase in energy efficiency also happens when people, companies, governments, and organizations opt for using more sustainable products: a city can electrify its fleet of buses, for example. Below are examples of optimizations with high potential leverage:

§  More efficient cement, steel, aluminium, and fertilizer production.

§  Retrofitting buildings for increased energy efficiency.

§  The use of sustainable materials as a design choice.

§  Infrastructure and products designed for longevity.

§  Recycling and reusing materials.

§  More fuel-efficient container ships, cars, trucks, and planes.

§  Smart management of district heating and public lights.

§  Providing an efficient public transport network and bike lanes.

§  Optimized supply chains.

§  Efficient cooling of data centers.

The implementation of these solutions is often limited by investment cost and the resistance of systems in place to change. To incentivize change, innovation can play a role by providing a wider range of better and cheaper solutions, while governments can support initial investments. Finally, energy intensity is always tight to the Jevons paradox: as a products become more energy-efficient, their cost can be reduced, leading to more consumption, and offsetting the energy gain. For example, making airplanes more fuel-efficient might translate into a reduced ticket price for the consumer, which in turn leads to more people flying…

2 Reducing carbon intensity (CO2 / Energy)

The carbon intensity represents how much CO2 is emitted to produce a unit of energy. Reducing carbon intensity involves shifting our energy mix from fossil fuels to clean energy sources.

Clean alternatives to fossil fuels include hydropower, solar, wind, biomass, geothermal and nuclear power. The challenge lies in making these clean energy sources cheaper than fossil fuels, and in the logistics of implementing a global energy transition. This objective involves developing the clean energy market and choosing the right clean energy source to employ in a given context. We should consider each energy source based on whether it is clean (carbon-free), renewable, energy dense, safe, cheap (both in terms of investment and operation costs), and available.

·        Hydro: Dams are great, but we cannot build many more of them. On the upside, they can serve as energy storage tanks by working in reverse (pumping water downstream into the reservoir). They can create issues around the control of fresh water (see the case of Ethiopia).

·        Wind: Wind turbines are getting cheaper and larger – thus more efficient. They can be built offshore where wind conditions are more predictable. To compensate intermittency, which can be daily and seasonal, wind turbines are best combined with energy storage systems or peaking power plants.

·        Nuclear: Nuclear plants can yield massive amounts of energy 24 hours a day. They require relatively little space and raw materials. We need to safely handle radioactive waste and make the power plants safe. Many new reactors designs have been proposed, that could be safer and produce less waste – for example, reactors based on liquid fuel and Thorium. Limitations to nuclear power include the time and investment costs involved to build new plants and public opposition.

·        Biomass: Biomass can be a carbon-neutral source of energy when it does not involve net deforestation. There are several “generations” of biofuels: those issued from agricultural products such as sugarcane or corn involve direct competition with the food sector and significant land usage. This is less the case of biofuels of second generation produced from woody biomass and residues. Finally, there are biofuels produced from algae which could become viable in the near future. Due to their high volumetric energy density, biofuels could, in the long run, substitute fossil fuels in airplanes and ships.

·        Solar: Solar energy is great, but it requires space and storage capacity to compensate for daily and seasonal intermittency and to transport energy. There is still a lot of exploitable space for solar PV on roofs in urbanized areas. Many tropical countries, countries in the Middle East, and Australia receive incredible amounts of sunlight; it really makes sense to build solar power plants there!

It is clear that a successful energy transition will involve composing with the benefits and downsides of each technology. The scale of a global transition is considerable: today, about 80% of our energy comes from the combustion of fossil fuels. Reducing this fraction to zero will take many years, even with consistently high investments in the energy transition, and especially in a context where much of the world has yet to complete its human development cycle. Finally, it seems that we will need to electrify as much of the transport sector as possible and many industrial processes without emitting extra CO2. The parts of industry that cannot be electrified (for example transport via airplanes and container ships) will have to run on hydrogen, biofuels, synthetic fuels, metal fuels or electrofuels produced using electricity. Developing effective grid energy storage solutions will be essential to support the exploitation of solar and wind technologies. It seems that electrical batteries are not going to be enough there, and the large-scale use of hydrogen is also questionable. This leaves a lot of room for innovation regarding which “storage mix” to use in combination with the future clean energy grid.

3 Carbon capture and storage

The idea of CCS is to adapt existing fossil fuel power plants such as gas power plants to enable the capture of CO2 before it gets released and to pump it back underground. At the center of the issue is the facts that this technology is expensive, while it reduces the capacity of a power plant significantly. Therefore, it must be “enforced actively” as part of a decarbonization effort, and the cost typically supported by the government. This could make sense in certain contexts, as the technology can be adapted to existing plants and there are some benefits to keeping gas power plants in the energy mix (low land usage, reliable power generation, no harmful waste). However, one should wonder if other strategies such as introducing a carbon tax or opting for other clean electricity sources might be more relevant options.

4 Carbon removal

Even if we eventually transit to a fully clean energy system, the transition will take many years to complete. Moreover, there are factors other than fossil fuels burning that contribute to GHG emissions, such as the emissions coming from deforestation and cows producing methane. By deforesting areas of land, the carbon stored in pre-existing biomass is released. Moreover, the amount of methane in the atmosphere is approximately in equilibrium with the livestock raised on Earth, which is rapidly increasing following the demand for meat-based products. The main avenues to remove excess CO2 from the atmosphere are through direct air capture (DAC) and reforestation. The problem with DAC is that it requires energy – which must be itself produced from clean energy sources. Moreover, DAC plants have yet to demonstrate large scale deployment. Similarly, planting trees is easy but making sure that the planted trees thrive and mature in the long run is hard. The main scepticism towards the relevance of carbon removal stems from that, in many cases, it makes more sense to avoid emitting CO2 in the first place rather than emitting it and paying for its removal, and to avoid deforesting rather than deforesting in one place and paying for reforestation in another.

5 Geoengineering

Geoengineering refers to the idea of artificially cooling the planet, for example by pumping sulphites into the atmosphere or dissolving massive amounts of olivine into the ocean. Most experts agree that geoengineering would only be a last resort solution to prevent the climate system from running out of control. Geoengineering might be a worthwhile area to study in order to understand exactly what is feasible, however it probably makes more sense to direct investments to clean technologies and to support decarbonization strategies.

Final notes and conclusion

Decarbonization is a complex multifaceted challenge and addressing it will require a lot of innovation and coordination between international organizations, governments, companies, and consumers. We should avoid oversimplifications such as :

·        Capitalism is the issue

·        Overpopulation is the issue

·        China is the issue

·        Rich people are the issue

·        Fossil fuel lobbies are the issue

·        People not changing their habits is the issue

·        The government should solve the problem

·        Technology will save us

·        God will save us

·        Just build solar plants in the desert and produce hydrogen

·        Just build nuclear plants

·        Just plant trees

·        Just buy less stuff

·        There is no problem in the first place; it’s all media sensationalism!

In reality, part of the solution lies in optimizing existing systems and design new products, infrastructures, and industrial processes towards energy efficiency. Another part is to complete a global energy transition by progressively retiring fossil fuels to the benefit of clean energy sources. And finally, we can question our initial definition of what constitutes a “high standard of living” and reconsider our lifestyle and attitude towards consumption. Overall, the climate problem is of fractal complexity and decarbonization is only one part of it.
